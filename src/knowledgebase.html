itris Knowledgebase

Product design, Interaction design
2022

Context

High quality documentation is an important cornerstone of any software product, particularly if the product contains some complex workflows. Having easily accessible, well maintained and structured documentation provides some key benefits to a business including:

  • Quicker onboarding for new users
  • Seamless learning experiences and reduction in learning curve
  • Increased trust in the company
  • Competitive advantage

Ultimately if users feel confident that they can use a product, they are more likely to be advocates and it dramatically reduces support and training calls. When a user feels confident that they know how to use a product, satisfaction increases, as does their performance. That was the objective when creating the itris Knowledgebase but it did not come without it's challenges.

I led the design and collaborated with key stakeholders from across the business to produce a high-quality learning resource for the company and the users of the product.

Problem

At the time, documentation existed as PDFs on a shared drive. Users did have access to these documents, but they were often difficult to find for users as well as itris staff. As a result, documents were often duplicated and changed to align more closely with the department consuming them which led to scattered documentation that was quickly out of date.

This gave rise to an increasing amount of inconsistency for users as there was not an easily accessible, central source of truth for learning materials. Additionally, the training team was also offering training courses but these were big commitments from customers and proved to be inefficient for an industry that typically have high staff turnover.

This was something that needed to be addressed as it was becoming an issue for the business and leading to users not getting the expected level of service they expected. It was also not meeting the standards of quality set by the business.

Goals

User

  • Easily accessible, high-quality learning materials for self-education
  • Sign-posting in the product for features with more advanced concepts
  • Written and video materials so that users can learn at their own pace in their preferred method

Business

  • Remove friction for self-educating for users
  • Increase user trust in the product and the company
  • Single source of truth repository for documentation
  • Lighten the burden on customer success and training teams
  • Promote learning opportunities in the product

Research

I looked into options of using an off-the-shelf solution to documentation and researched products like GitBook and Notion, but they lacked the customisability that we needed. I also looked into whether we could expose our Confluence articles publicly, but this came with too many trade-offs and again, a lack of customisation.

I then looked at some best-in-class documentation for some inspiration and found myself drawn to Stripe Guides in particular. I also looked at Apple's support docs as well as Atlassian's documentation.

Opportunity

Since none of the off-the-shelf solutions were viable, I explored the idea of creating our own purpose-built site that catered for the different learning journeys for a user and somewhere users could also find top tips, FAQs and product release notes.

Challenges

  • All of the learning materials were currently in PDFs and our internal docs platform so these would need to be ported over to the site (which was in markdown format)
  • A new iteration of our visual language was in progress but it was immature so implementing this would add to the scope as new components would need to be created specifically for the knowledgebase
  • The training team would need to learn and become familiar with VS Code, writing articles in markdown and have a base understanding of git version control.

Solution

I spent some time exploring different designs and after many different routes and meetings with stakeholders, I settled on a final version of the designs. Throughout the ideation phase, it was important to ensure that the design felt right and was aligned with the brand.

The final product contained a landing page, articles accessible through a series route (mirroring training courses) or categories route, search capabilities, release notes, top tips and FAQs.

Results

The knowledgebase was a success and received positive feedback from users and stakeholders (and even a competitor). Since its release in 2022, there are 200+ articles on the site, as well as 50+ top tips and 60+ FAQs.

Monthly traffic to the knowledgebase is X and the number of training and support enquiries has dropped by nearly 50%.

As a result of the knowledgebase, the demands on our services changed and the volume of inbound calls and emails reduced considerably. We were also able to reduce the training function's headcount from 5 members of staff down to 1, whilst still maintaining strong customer feedback. Ian Farrow, Product Director
